Exploring the complex issue of "Pretendians" in Canada through the lens of Drew Hayden Taylor's CBC documentary. The documentary explores fraudlent claims of Indigenous identity in Canada, highlighting the impact on genuine First Nations, Métis, and Inuit communities.

About the Film

Hosted by Anishinaabe author, Drew Hayden Taylor, The Pretendians explores the growing issue of people falsely claiming Indigenous heritage often for professional gain, status, or resources. The documentary takes viewers on a cross-Canada journey, revealing the strange and sometimes absurd world of "race shifters." From fake Indigenous art to entire fake communities, Taylor investigates why this is happening now and how it impacts genuine Indigenous peoples.

Short Profile Drew Hayden Taylor An award-winning playwright, novelist, columnist, and filmmaker from the Curve Lake First Nations in Ontario. Known for his ability to tackle serious Indigenous issues with humor, Taylor brings a unique perspective to this investigation.

    Persuasive Techniques

    Visual With experiments like the Wine experiment, where he mixes drops of Red wine into clear wine to observe how much quantity of small drops can change the whole outlook or content of the wine.
    Tone The film moves from an emotional arc of curiosity and absurdity to deep emotional pain and anger, guiding to the audience to understand the severity of the issue.
    Ethos Taylor switches from first person narrative to expert analysis involving demographers like Lisa Dillon and journalists like Francesca Fionda. This shifts the case from individual image to a collective view, making the information more trustworthy.
    Logos Lisa Dillon reveals that from the year of 2000, there has been an increase from 15,000 to 70,000 of people identifying as Eastern Metis. On the other hand, Francesca Fionda states that 75% of shops checked from an audit sold inauthentic art. This information helps the audience analyze the issue with tangible real world statistics.
    Pathos Taylor advances from analysts to personal stories, creating an emotional connection with the audience through individual experiences. This raises humanitarian concerns, evoking a basis of understanding of the impacts and harms caused by fraudulent identity claims.
    The documentary effectively combines visual storytelling, emotional tone, and appeals to ethos, logos, and pathos to engage viewers and convey the serious implications of fraudulent Indigenous identity claims.

    FNMI Perspectives

  • Ancestral Knowledge and Oral History: Drew Hayden Taylor emphasizes the importance of understanding ancestral knowledge, and laws, before creating art. He highlights that this deep understanding is crucial for carving, for example, totem poles, and that this knowledge is passed down through generations.
  • Indigenous peoples resilience: The documentary implicitly celebrates the resilience of Indigenous peoples in the face of historical injustices, including attempts at cultural erasure. Lyanna Constantino of TAFF speaks about how "pretendians" distort Indigenous cultures, while also highlighting the ongoing fight for recognition and rights, as seen in the efforts against fraudulent claims.

    • The cost of Appropriation

  • Resource Theft: Grants, scholarships, jobs, and awards meant for Indigenous people are taken by those with false claims, depriving actual community members of opportunities.
  • Misrepresentation:Pretendians often become spokespeople, shaping public perception and policy with voices that are not authentically Indigenous.
  • Trauma and trust:These cases cause deep pain and mistrust within communities, reopening wounds of colonial erasure and displacement.
  • Undermines Indigenous CultureFake art without genuine connection to Indigenous culture also undermines the meaning and message of the artwork to the community.

    Challenging Assumptions

    Through understanding the systems in which injustices are forced onto Indigenous peoples, Taylor tackles and challenges on-going false societal beliefs that portray Indigenous identity and culture in a different way far from the truth.

    Myth

    Indigenous identity can be determined by appearance or a small amount of "blood quantum."

    Reality

    Through the wine experiment, the documentary challenges this thinking by highlighting that appearance and blood quantum are not reliable indicators of Indigenous identity.

    Myth

    Claiming distant Indigenous ancestry from centuries ago is sufficient to identify as Indigenous today.

    Reality

    Genealogist Lisa Dylan explains that while many French Canadians have a distant Indigenous ancestor, this ancestor represents a very small percentage of their overall genetic makeup. The documentary visually represents this as one name out of several thousand ancestors over 10 generations.

    Myth

    Being of mixed Indigenous and non-Indigenous heritage automatically makes someone Métis.

    Reality

    The documentary clarifies that being Métis involves a connection to a historic Métis community that has continued into the present day.

    Myth

    Institutions are inclusive by not questioning claims.

    Reality

    Failure to verify identity undermines Tribal Sovereignty.

    Myth

    Marrying an Indigenous person grants Indigenous status and benefits.

    Reality

    The documentary discusses how, prior to 1985, white women who married Indigenous men were granted status, which they retained even after divorce or the husband's death. However, some, like Angela Lavali, argue that this should not entitle non-Indigenous spouses to the same benefits as those who are Indigenous by birth and have lived that experience.

    A Call to Action

    The path forward requires The path forward requires everyone’s efforts and true submission. From the general public, to consumers, to the institutions and individuals with genuine heritage but no community connection. The audience is encouraged to support Indigenous led initiatives to foster cultural continuity and call out the government to operate by the 94 Calls To Action.

    View Action Items
    1. For Consumers, buy Authentic Indigenous Art. Shoppers are urged to be discerning and to research products to ensure they are buying authentic Indigenous art and souvenirs. This includes looking for labels that identify the artist, their community, and whether they are receiving royalties.
    2. For Institutions and Universities, address Fraudulent Claims. Universities and institutions are called upon to take action against individuals falsely claiming Indigenous identity, especially when it impacts academic integrity, scholarships, and research funding. The documentary suggests they should follow the example of the University of Saskatchewan in dismissing individuals found to be "pretend Indians".
    3. For Society at Large, respect Indigenous Identity and Culture. The overarching call to action is for people to respect Indigenous identity and culture and to stop the practice of falsely claiming it. The documentary emphasizes that "pretendians" are not only perpetuating fraud but also distorting and erasing Indigenous cultures.
    4. For Individuals with Genuine Indigenous Heritage, Validate Claims and Connect with Communities: The documentary implicitly encourages those who genuinely have Indigenous heritage but may have lost connection to actively research and connect with their communities for validation and belonging.
    5. Draw Lines: As Drew Hayden Taylor states at the end, "people, you have to draw the line somewhere". This calls for a collective understanding and enforcement of boundaries against appropriation and misrepresentation. The film concludes with a plea from Drew Hayden Taylor to be understood and accepted, but not to be impersonated.

    Discussions

    After watching the documentary, you are encouraged to reflect and raise discussions about each topic or point that is raised in the documentary. Below are start-up questions that can help in guiding these discussions:

    • The film explores the emotional toll of being accused of being a "pretendian" and the stress it causes. What are the ethical considerations when publicly questioning someone's identity?
    • What are the challenges faced by Indigenous individuals who have lost connection to their ancestral communities? How can these connections be rebuilt?
    • What does the desire to be Indigenous, as explored in the film, reveal about a perceived "identity gap" or a need for cultural sustenance in mainstream society?
    • Why do you think there has been a rise in people claiming Indigenous identity in recent decades?
    • The video discusses "native on native" cultural appropriation. Can Indigenous individuals or groups appropriate from other Indigenous cultures?
